# Block 1 — Ontological Shift
### Identity Navigation Stack — Workshop 1: Reading the Field
### Duration: 45 min

---

## What this block does

Before you can build a system that reads identity topology, you need to see clearly what you are currently building instead.

This block has no slides. It is an exercise followed by a minimal theoretical frame.  
The exercise comes first — before the theory — because the theory only lands after you have felt the problem in your own hands.

**Materials needed:**
- The input text below (printed or shared on screen)
- `handouts/dmes_reference.md` — do not open until Part 3

---

## Part 1 — The exercise (10 min)

Read this input. It comes from a real conversation.

---

*"I've been in this role for about three years now. It's a good company, genuinely smart people, and the work is interesting — at least the technical side of it. But lately I've been feeling like something isn't quite right. I keep thinking about a startup I almost joined two years ago. I turned it down because the timing wasn't great — we'd just had a kid, the salary cut would have been significant. They did well, actually. Not life-changing well, but well. And now there's something similar on the table again. Different company, earlier stage, higher risk. I'm not sure if I'm drawn to it because it's actually right, or because I'm just restless. My partner is supportive either way, which almost makes it harder."*

---

**Your task:** Write the next three questions or prompts you would send this person if you were building a system to help them.

Write them down. Do not discuss yet.

Take 5 minutes. Write exactly three.

---

## Part 2 — Classification (15 min)

Share your questions in the group. Write them on a board or shared doc.

Now classify each question using this grid:

| Type | What it does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Clarifying** | Requests more data about what was said | "How long has the new opportunity been on the table?" |
| **Categorising** | Tries to place the person in a framework | "Would you describe yourself as risk-tolerant?" |
| **Solving** | Moves toward a recommendation or resolution | "Have you mapped out the financial runway if you take the cut?" |
| **Topographic** | Reads the structure of the field above the content | *(see below)* |

Count how many of each type are on the board.

**What you will find:** almost all questions are clarifying, categorising, or solving. This is not a failure — it is the architecture of every system you have been trained to build. Query → response. Problem → solution. Input → output.

The fourth type — topographic — is probably absent or rare. If someone wrote one, examine it together: what made it different?

---

## What a topographic move looks like

A topographic move is not a question about the content. It is a hypothesis about the structure of the field — expressed as a situation or invitation, not as a request for information.

**Clarifying:** *"What exactly happened with the first startup?"*  
→ Requests data. Fills in the content map.

**Categorising:** *"Are you someone who needs external pressure to make decisions?"*  
→ Asks the person to self-report on a trait. Self-report is not topographic data.

**Solving:** *"It sounds like the financial risk is the main barrier — have you modelled it?"*  
→ Presupposes what the problem is and moves toward resolution. Closes the field prematurely.

**Topographic:** *"You said your partner being supportive almost makes it harder. Stay with that — what does harder feel like?"*  
→ Does not request data. Does not categorise. Does not solve. It follows the movement of the field toward something that was named and immediately compressed. The compression is the signal.

Or:

**Topographic:** *"Imagine you took the role and it went well — really well. Two years in, you're glad you did it. What specifically are you glad about?"*  
→ A future simulation probe. Not asking what they want — activating the configuration so the D-vector can become visible through what they elaborate.

The difference is architectural. The first three types process what is said. The fourth reads what the pattern of saying reveals.

## Part 3 — Minimal theory (15 min)

Now open `handouts/dmes_reference.md`.

Three concepts to anchor before Block 2:

**Field, not profile.**  
A profile is a stable set of attributes. A field is a dynamic topology — configurations that activate differently under different conditions. The same person has different DMES coordinates in different Faces. The system is not building a profile. It is mapping a field.

**Face as attractor, not type.**  
A Face is not a personality type. It is a configuration the field returns to after perturbation. It is visible not in what a person says — but in what persists across varied conditions. The input text above contains at least one Face signal. It appears in what the person compresses, not what they expand.

*(Discuss: what was compressed? — "My partner is supportive either way, which almost makes it harder." One sentence. High Meaning load. The field moved there and away in the same breath.)*

**Probe as hypothesis, not question.**  
The system's next move is always a hypothesis about an uncharted zone of the topology — expressed as a situation or invitation that would activate the configuration needed to test the hypothesis. Not: "what do you value?" But: a scenario that would reveal value through movement, not declaration.

---

## Part 4 — Rewrite (5 min)

Take one of your original three questions from Part 1.  
Rewrite it as a topographic move.

You now have the vocabulary. Use it.

Share one rewrite in the room. The group asks: is this topographic, or is it clarifying/categorising/solving in new clothes?

This is harder than it looks. That difficulty is the point.

---

## What you leave Block 1 with

A felt sense — not just a conceptual understanding — of the difference between query-response logic and topographic logic.

You cannot build the architecture in Block 3 without this shift. The system prompt, the probe algorithm, the stopping criterion — all of it is meaningless if the underlying logic is still query-response.

Block 2 will give you the signal vocabulary. Block 3 will give you the architecture. But the shift happens here, in the gap between the questions you wrote and the questions you didn't.
